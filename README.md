# Talk Board

A communication board for someone with aphasia. Big buttons with pictures and words; every tap is spoken aloud and shown huge on screen. Works on iPad, iPhone, and any laptop browser. Works offline once loaded.

**Nothing personal lives in this code or on the server.** Family names, photos, and saved phrases are stored only on the device they were added on — they are never uploaded.

## Set up the iPad (2 minutes)

1. Open the site URL in **Safari**.
2. Tap the **Share** button → **Add to Home Screen** → Add. It becomes a full-screen app called "Talk".
3. Open it once while online — after that it works without internet.
4. Tap any button once so the iPad "unlocks" speech (iOS requires one tap before it will speak).
5. Optional but recommended — **Guided Access** keeps him in the app so a stray tap can't leave it:
   Settings → Accessibility → Guided Access → On. Then open Talk Board and triple-click the top button to lock in.

Repeat step 1–2 on your phone and laptop. Add people/photos on each device you want them on (they don't sync — no server).

## Family/carer bits

- **⚙️ gear (bottom-right)** — Settings:
  - **Simple mode** for harder days: just Yes/No + six essential buttons.
  - **Full mode** for better moments: pain locator, feelings, people, typing, choices.
  - Add **People** with photos and names.
  - Pick the **voice** and speed (Karen, the Australian voice, is used automatically if the iPad has it — Settings → Accessibility → Spoken Content → Voices to download more).
- **Choices** — you type 2–4 options ("Water / Tea", "Now / Later"), tap *Show him the choices*, and hand him the screen. You carry the words; he just picks. This is the most useful tool on days when finding words is hardest.
- **Yes / No** are always at the top of every screen.
- **Pain** asks where, then how bad, and speaks a full sentence ("My back hurts. It's very bad.").

## Tips for talking with aphasia

- Ask one question at a time, yes/no where possible.
- Give him time — the words are there even when they won't come out.
- If speech fails, hand him the board; if the board fails, offer two choices with your hands apart and let him point.
- His understanding may be better than his output — keep talking *to* him normally.
